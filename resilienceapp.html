The Power Of Communication

By David Guindon

One day, I decided to do some volunteer design work to help the COVID-19 effort. After I got access to all the required files and channels, I jumped right in. When I came on board the project, most of the initial planning had been done. There was an excellent set of Style Guides, UI Components, User Flows, and Personas! Other designers had already spent about a week coming up with ideas and wireframes. I spent the next two days looking over the non-wireframe Information Architecture. I also attended a few designer meetings to get a better feel for what was going on. Now I was ready to start knocking out kanban cards!

About 50 kanban cards were marked for designers at this point, and around 15 of them were marked as completed. "Not a bad start," I thought. I picked a card that sounded fun and opened up the Figma file. There were at LEAST 500 frames in there, and on initial inspection, they were in no particular order. "Oh man!" I thought, "this one might be tough. It looks like an army of designers all sprinted to a wall and started writing wherever there was space!" Creating any new ideations at this point would just be making the mess bigger. I spent the next two days looking over what had already been created and mentally sorting out what frames went with what kanban card and what screens could be effectively linked.

After looking everything over, I noticed that a kanban card could have up to five solutions, all by different designers, whose names did not appear under the designer tab. However, nearly all the solutions were effective in the context of the kanban card.

This is what I think was happening. When the project was fresh, several designers joined each day. They read the onboarding instructions, looked over the provided information architecture, picked a kanban card, then wireframed a solution. However, they were too nervous to move kanban cards to the next phase. After a day or two, they would get overwhelmed and quit the project. What an interesting problem!

After a few more days and three long meetings, we ended up hashing out a vague path through all the content to make a viable initial MVP. Now that things settled down a bit, we were down to approximately 25 kanban cards without solutions. I grabbed a few that all dealt with roughly the same task, and went to work. I let the other designers who had been on the project longer polish up the designs we decided to use. I frequently used Figma's comment tool to share ideas and concerns about the unpolished cards, or the cards that someone else was editing.

After completing the cards I was working on, I started polishing the designs adjacent to what I had sent to the developers. That way, when someone had a question about that section, they would know whom to ask. After chatting with the devs for a while and watching my designs become a reality, the initial MVP shipped. It was a fun journey filled with amazing people from every time zone. The team's ability to talk out potential problems and mold a huge set of puzzle pieces into a cohesive story with so many hands in the design pie was a great experience. Soon after this, the next project came knocking, and I headed out.

Resilience App

Resilience Lowers the Burden of Planning for Volunteer Organizations, Delivering Goods to Those in Need.

Discovery & Research

All four personas required unique user flows and interfaces.

Maria Summers

  • Tier: Organizer
  • Age: 32
  • Occupation: Volunteer Coordinator
  • Location: Los Angeles, CA
  • Bio:
    Lives in LA, works for a volunteer organization that uses or app to coordinate their community efforts.
  • Goals:
    Maria wants to be able to communicate with stakeholders and organization team members. She also wants the information on location, availability, and capacity for each volunteer.
  • Frustrations:
    Maria is overwhelmed by the number of volunteers and requests from recipients. She needs to match volunteer availability, location, and capacity with recipient requests.

    • David Abagnale

  • Tier: Volunteer
  • Age: 25
  • Occupation: Construction
  • Location: Los Angeles, CA
  • Bio:
    David wants to give back to his community in his spare time to help those in need. He requires guidance from FeedFolksLA on how to do that.
  • Goals:
    David wants to help deliver food or pharmaceuticals to recipients. He needs to indicate availability, location, and capacity.
  • Frustrations:
    David can't help everyone, can he share missions on social media? He can only drive a certain distance. He can only take missions that fit his schedule.

    • Carl Joyce

  • Tier: Recipient
  • Age: 71
  • Occupation: Retired
  • Location: Los Angeles, CA
  • Bio:
    Carl lives alone, sheltering in place, retired, widowed, immunocompromised, and has family that lives too far away to help with food regularly.
  • Goals:
    Carl needs to request food directly from FeedFolksLA. The help must be reliable and regular.
  • Frustrations:
    Carl has a hard time using technology; requests may be time-sensitive. Carl has poor eyesight.

    • Debbie Balvin

  • Tier: Donor
  • Age: 57
  • Occupation: Business Owner
  • Location: Los Angeles, CA
  • Bio:
    Debbie runs a successful business; she wants to donate to organizations that help food-insecure folk.
  • Goals:
    Debbie needs to know which organizations need funding help. She wants to help someone like Carol buy groceries for the week.
  • Frustrations:
    Debbie doesn't know which missions need funding and how much. Can she make her donations automatically recurrent?

    • Information Architecture

    Initially, we were reskinning an existing relief app. However, it became clear early that starting from scratch would solve our user's needs more effectively.

    Visual Design

    The blue and white design promotes stability, safety, and confidence. The cartoon pictures give healing and softness. Open Sans was chosen for its straightforward and friendly feel.

    My main focus in the project was the style guide, personas, reviewing co-worker designs, and giving feedback.

    I also designed the forms for the Organizer persona.

    Conclusion

    Working on ResilienceApp was an enlightening experience. However, I was unable to focus on making and testing my designs, which was disappointing. We had a team of 20+ designers that dropped down to about five. No leadership with the final say, coupled with user stories being broad and vague. This environment created many repeated features and conflicting design flows. However, design is not all about mockups and research. It is about solving problems. You never know what you are getting into when onboarding into a new project. Getting across the finish line always feels good.
