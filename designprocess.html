    • User stories are the first attempt a designer makes at trying to determine what the product needs to be in its purest form.
    • The user story is the document that sets the big picture goals for the project.
    • User stories are a good way to make sure scope creep is not introduced into the project.
    • User flows are used to map the steps of completing a specific task in an interface.
    • The hidden complexities of user tasks can be better understood by clearly visualizing their steps using a user flow.
    • A user flow can be used to find ways to make a task simpler by seeing all the components of a task laid out in an organized manner.
    • Wireframes are a collection of shapes that outline how a portion of a digital product could be designed.
    • Wireframes provide clarity into how information will be organized on the screen. The absence of finishing details allows you as a designer to focus on how the information is organized rather than what it will look like in its final form.
    • The more complex a design a project has, the more wireframes it will require. The goal is to create enough wireframes to test and convey your idea.
    • Brand characteristics pertains to all of the facets of a brand, not just its logo.
    • This refers to the feeling of the brand, who the main audience of the brand is, how the brand positions itself compared to its competitors, and how it tries to differentiate itself, among other things.
    • This is something that should be talked about early with the client so you both understand how the brand is trying to express itself.
    • Color has many uses, but what we focus on in UX design is that the colors you choose convey a feeling and help specific things stand out.
    • A big reason why you want to create a color palette is to keep larger projects from having lots of similar colors. Instead of having twenty five or more colors of red, it would be better to limit it to two to four. This can be achieved by having everyone on a project choose from a premade list.
    • When creating a style guide you want to stick with a specific style of color chord. Examples of a few color chords are Complimentry, Analogous, and Triadic
    • When choosing typography for a project, having a plan for size and style is important so that it stays consistant throughout a project.
    • This keeps the feel of the project the same, and saves time when designing a large ammount of content for a project.
    • Creating a guide for typography is a good practice for any project.
    • I start by hand sketching logos, then quickly transition to the computer.
    • It is important to have multiple variations of a logo, because it may be printed on black and white stationary, in full color, or even on something like a pen or mug.
    • Logos tend to have whitespace guidelines that let you know what the spacing around the logo should be.
    • Hi Fidelity Mockups are where you add more detail to your wireframe, incorporating the brand characteristics you created earlier.
    • Like wireframes, you need to make enough of these to get a feel for the project. Most projects will have one sheet for each possible screen.
    • Large projects may not have a sheet for each screen, but rather a template to make each needed variation.
    • A clickable prototype lets you get user feedback without having to create a full working version of the project.
    • This step is very helpful for a project that is expensive to build, and requires multiple runs of testing and tweaking.
    • There are many types of applications that let you create clickable prototypes.
    • Preference testing is when you have a group of potential users give you feedback on what they like best out of a list of possible choices.
    • When you have several variations of an element and need to know which one would be best for a given demographic, preference testing is what you want to do.
    • I would suggest limiting the choices down to four or less when performing preference testing.
    • Usability tests are when you have someone navigate through a clickable prototype and give you feedback.
    • It is helpful to take notes and or record the session. Asking the test taker to speak their thoughts out loud and to not worry about hurting anyone's feelings can be very helpful.
    • This is where I find myself able to tweak the project most effectvely because the feedback can be very eye opening.
    • A deliverable package is your final version of the project.
    • This could be for a client, for someone else also working on the project, or it could be a presentation.
    • The deliverable package should be neatly organized, include everything needed to create or use the package, and also have a way to contact you if needed.
